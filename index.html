Abstract

       Music therapy is non-pharmaceutical medical treatment that uses the different aspects of music to help treat different types of 
       mental disorders. 
      
       Music can stimulate different types of emotions in people, which stimulate brain signals. This kind of therapy aims to find out what 
       rhythms evoke certain signals to see whether they can be produced again through technology and engineering to help the patient 
       feel the same feeling. 
      
       This is particularly useful when treating mental disorders such as depression, anxiety, obsessive compulsive disorder as you see which 
       melodies / rhythms trigger positive emotions and then attempt to reproduce this. Researchers tend to look for patterns in a 
       patient’s behaviour e.g., elevated heart rate / sounds, EEG recordings etc. Depending on the reading for this, you can tell whether it improved 
       the patient’s mood or not. If researchers group together, they may see trends in different groups of patients.

Introduction

      Music therapy is the non-pharmaceutical treatment of illnesses, diseases and disorders. Music can access prefrontal, limbic and 
      sensory pathways. This then triggers dopamine receptors as well as stress-related hormone responses, which can affect mood and cognitive function.
      
      It is able to target fields like emotional development or cognitive development. So, the therapy process and devices involved are dependent on 
      what you want to treat. For example, if a therapist wanted to focus on speech and language development, they’d be looking at lyrics, rhythmic 
      movement analysis, percussive instruments and their timbres and more (Das, Gupta and Neogi, 2023).

Analysis and discussion

 
      
 What devices are used? 

      Devices have been engineered to obtain results which are useful for various areas in music therapy. 
      A common device used is an electroencephalogram (EEG), which is used to record brain activity and its signals when performing different tasks. 
      It is made up of electrodes that are embedded into a silicon cap. This is placed on the patient’s head to detect charges from brain cells, which 
      produce a brain wave pattern. From there, they are interpreted to help diagnose conditions like narcolepsy (Cleveland Clinic, 2021).
    
      As technology develops, scientists are trying to develop more causal devices to take recordings. For example, smart watches can be used
      to take someone’s pulse, and heart rate variability (HRV). HRV can be used to gage how well the body can adapt to stress and recovering
      from it (Cleveland Clinic, 2021). A low HRV would suggest that someone responds worse to stress, hence it being common amongst people with anxiety. Research has shown that music can 
      divert a patient’s attention from negative emotions. This would stimulate auditory receptors due to less activity in the sympathetic nervous 
      system also known as the “fight or flight” system (Kučikienė and Praninskienė, 2018).
